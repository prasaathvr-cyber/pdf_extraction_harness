    "Treatment discontinuation with methylphenidate in adults with attention deficit hyperactivity disorder: a meta-analysis of randomized clinical trials\n\nXavier Castells · Ruth Cunill · Dolors Capellà\n\nReceived: 28 February 2012 / Accepted: 5 August 2012 / Published online: 16 September 2012\n© Springer-Verlag 2012\n\nAbstract\nBackground Attention deficit hyperactivity disorder (ADHD) in adulthood is increasingly diagnosed and treated. Methylphenidate is frequently advocated as a first-line pharmacological treatment.\n\nPurpose The aim of our study was to compare all-cause discontinuation rate of methylphenidate and its pharmaceutical presentations with placebo in adults with ADHD.\n\nMethods This was a systematic review and meta-analysis of randomized controlled trials comparing methylphenidate with placebo in adults with ADHD. All-cause treatment discontinuation was the primary endpoint. The efficacy in reducing ADHD symptoms and safety were the secondary endpoints.\n\nResults Twelve studies (2,496 patients) met the inclusion criteria. Four racemic methylphenidate and one dexmethylphenidate presentations were investigated. The rate of all-cause treatment discontinuation was greater with methylphenidate than with placebo, but this difference was not statistically significant [odds ratio (OR) 1.19, 95 % confidence interval (95 % CI) 0.82–1.74, P=0.37, I²=64 %] This finding reached the conventional threshold of statistical significance after one outlier study was excluded (OR 1.44, 95 % CI 1.14–1.82, P=0.002, I²=0). Methylphenidate was more efficacious than placebo for reducing ADHD symptoms and it was associated with a higher proportion of patients dropping out due to adverse effects.\n\nConclusions Despite reducing ADHD symptoms, methylphenidate showed no advantage over placebo in terms of treatment discontinuation. More attention should be given in the future to the endpoint \"all-cause treatment discontinuation\" when making regulatory decisions and developing clinical guidelines involving the treatment of ADHD in adulthood.\n\nKeywords Attention deficit hyperactivity disorder · Adulthood · Methylphenidate · All-cause treatment discontinuation · Meta-analysis\n\nIntroduction\n\nAttention deficit hyperactivity disorder (ADHD) is a chronic childhood onset disorder that persists into adulthood with an estimated prevalence of around 2.5–4 % [1–3]. It has been associated with increased risk of psychiatric co-morbid disorders, particularly mood, anxiety, personality and substance use disorders (SUD) [4–6], car accidents [7] and social impairment [8]. Numerous studies have shown that treatment with methylphenidate results in improved ADHD symptoms [9–11] and appears to be safe with, in general, mild and transient side effects [12–14]. Methylphenidate is, therefore, frequently advocated as a first-line pharmacological treatment for adult ADHD in recently published reviews and clinical guidelines [14–18], particularly methylphenidate long-acting formulations, which simplify dosing regimens [19]. Consequently, a notable rise in methylphenidate use for",

    "Statistical analysis\nThe odds ratio (OR) was calculated for each study outcome.\nThe population included in these calculations was the ran-\ndomized one. Studies with multiple methylphenidate dose\ngroups were combined into a single group and included in\nthe meta-analysis as one comparison [37]. To combine\nparallel-group studies with cross-over ones, we attempted to\napply Elburne’s recommendations [38], but the available data\ndid not allow us to perform the analysis as planned, and a post\nhoc decision was made to limit the meta-analysis to parallel-\ngroup clinical trials. Weighted averages and 95% confidence\nintervals (95 %CI) were calculated by means of the inverse\nvariance method. Statistical heterogeneity was investigated by\nmeans of the chi-square test and the I2 measure, which deter-\nmines the proportion of variability due to heterogeneity [37].\nData were pooled using the random effect model. These\nanalyses were carried out for each methylphenidate and dex-\nmethylphenidate formulation and for all of them together. The\ncontour-enhanced funnel plot and the Egger’s test for detect-\ning skewness in the funnel plot were applied to detect publi-\ncation bias. This finding was particularly clear for OROS methylphenidate.\n\nRegulatory agencies and clinical guideline developers make their decisions and recommendations after weighing the benefits and risks of the intervention under evaluation. Our study shows that methylphenidate is more efficacious in reducing ADHD symptom severity than placebo, but it is also associated with a higher AE-induced discontinuation rate. Given these data, it is difficult to balance methylphenidate efficacy and safety, primarily because efficacy is based on subjective outcomes, which are difficult to appraise, instead of on objective and clinically meaningful ones [26, 27]. When no information on clinical remission is provided, all-cause treatment discontinuation rate can be a helpful study endpoint to assist in the selection of drugs; this endpoint could also be considered for regulatory decision-making [35]. Our analysis indicates that methylphenidate, and particularly its OROS formulation, may be associated with a higher all-cause treatment discontinuation rate than placebo. As such, our results warn of the possibility that this drug may have an unsuitable risk–benefit ratio for the treatment of adults with ADHD. This debate is particularly valid today. OROS methylphenidate was licensed in the US for the treatment of ADHD in adults in 2008 and is currently under assessment for this indication in EU countries.\n\nThere appear to be two possible explanations for our finding that the only methylphenidate formulation showing statistically significant negative results for all-cause and AE-induced treatment discontinuation was the OROS formulation. The first one lies in its pharmacological characteristics. OROS methylphenidate is the formulation with the longest lasting effects [55]. This property has been claimed to be an advantage because it provides a suitable pharmacological profile for a once a day treatment. However, the longer the effect, the more likely and more enduring the side effects are likely to be, thereby increasing the possibility of patients discontinuing treatment. With twice-daily or three times daily therapeutic regimens, like those used with the ER, SODAS or IR formulations, it is easier to adjust the duration of effects as necessary. A statistical explanation may also be possible. The fact that the size of the patient population",

    "Limitations\nStudy limitations are closely related to the quality of the\nincluded studies [28]. Our study highlights the difficulty\nof carrying out clinical trials that are free of the risk of\nbias when methylphenidate is investigated. Two princi-\npal sources of bias were identified. The first is blinding\nfailure, which may yield performance and detection\nbias. This can happen when medications with powerful\nbehavioral and hemodynamic effects, such as methyl-\nphenidate, are compared to placebo. The possibility of\nblinding failure stresses the importance of using objec-\ntive outcomes to limit the influence of performance and\ndetection bias instead of relying on subjective outcomes,\nsuch as ADHD symptom improvement. The second\npotential source of bias is attrition bias. Most studies\nhad high discontinuation rates, making attrition bias\nrather likely (except for all-cause treatment discontinua-\ntion, which is not affected by this bias). Additionally,\nthe short duration of the studies included in our meta-\nanalysis limits the validity of this study, while the\nlimited sample size of some RPCCT reduces the preci-\nsion of the estimations of some analyses.\nWe could not combine cross-over studies with parallel-\ngroup studies as originally planned. Therefore, we decided\nto include only studies with parallel-group design. The\nexclusion of cross-over trials may have reduced the statisti-\ncal power of this study. However, given that only two small\nstudies had this design, it is likely that this decision has had\nonly a small influence on the precision of the effect\nestimates.\nReporting bias can jeopardize the validity of any\nmeta-analysis. In our study, the funnel plots showed\nsymmetrical distribution and provided no evidence for\nsmall study effects on the main study endpoint, there-\nfore suggesting that reporting bias was unlikely. The\nfact that several bibliographic and clinical trials data-\nbases were checked could explain why the risk of\npublication bias in our study was low. Nevertheless,\nlack of efficacy-induced dropouts was available for\nabout one-third of studies; thus, our findings for this\nendpoint could be biased towards an overestimation of\nthe effect of methylphenidate.\nThe exclusion of clinical trials with a lead-in phase and\nthose conducted in an inpatient setting may be criticized.\nDuring the lead-in phase, non-compliant patients are exclud-\ned as well as placebo responders and patients intolerant to,\nor non-responders to, methylphenidate. As long as our pri-\nmary study outcome was all-cause treatment discontinua-\ntion, the inclusion of such studies could bias the results of\nour meta-analysis. Given that ADHD is mostly diagnosed\nand treated in the outpatient setting, studies that were not\nconducted in this setting were excluded.\nConclusions\nIn conclusion, methylphenidate showed no advantage over\nplacebo in terms of treatment discontinuation. In fact, the\nproportion of patients that dropped out for any reason was\nlarger amongst those that received methylphenidate. This\nfinding was particularly evident with the OROS formula-\ntion. Based on the results of our study, it is unclear whether\nmethylphenidate should be recommended as routine treat-\nment of adults with ADHD. More attention should be given\nto all-cause treatment discontinuation when investigating or\nappraising an intervention for this disorder. Larger sample\nsize and longer duration should be considered in future\nclinical trials to rule out the possibility of differences in\nall-cause treatment discontinuation rate between the studied\ninterventions.\nReferences\n1.
