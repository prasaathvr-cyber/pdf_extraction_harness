    "ORIGINAL CONTRIBUTION\n\nLong-Term Treatment Outcome in Adult Male Prisoners With Attention-Deficit/Hyperactivity Disorder\nThree-Year Naturalistic Follow-Up of a 52-Week Methylphenidate Trial\n\nYlva Ginsberg, MD, PhD,*† Niklas Långström, MD, PhD,†\nHenrik Larsson, PhD,† and Nils Lindefors, MD, PhD*\n\nAbstract: Despite high rates of attention-deficit/hyperactivity disorder (ADHD) among adult lawbreakers, particularly the long-term effects of ADHD pharmacotherapy remain unclear, not the least because of ethical challenges with preventing control subjects in randomized controlled trials from receiving medication over prolonged time. We followed up adult male prisoners with ADHD who completed a 5-week randomized, double-blind, placebo-controlled trial followed by a 47-week open-label extension of osmotic-release oral system methylphenidate in a Swedish high-security prison from 2007 to 2010 (ClinicalTrials.gov: NCT00482313). Twenty-five trial completers were prospectively followed up clinically 1 year (24/25, 96% participated fully or in part) and 3 years (20/25, 80% participation) after trial regarding ADHD symptoms (observer and self-reports), psychosocial functioning, substance misuse, and criminal reoffending. Methylphenidate-related improvements in ADHD symptoms and psychosocial functioning obtained during the 52-week trial were maintained at 1- and 3-year follow-ups. Specifically, after 3 years, 75% (15/20) of the respondents had been released from prison, and 67% of these (10/15) had employment, usually full time. In contrast, nonmedicated respondents at the 3-year follow-up (5/20) reported more ADHD symptoms, functional impairment, and substance misuse compared with currently medicated respondents (15/20). Further, 40% of the respondents self-reported reoffending, indicating a substantially lower relapse rate than expected (70%–80%).\n\nIn summary, although these observations need validation from new and larger samples, positive effects were maintained after 4 years of methylphenidate treatment. Most study completers were employed and had no relapse in substance misuse or criminality. These results suggest that motivational support and continued medication are important for improved outcome in adult criminal offenders with ADHD.\n\nKey Words: attention-deficit/hyperactivity disorder, stimulants, criminal offenders, reoffending, long-term outcome\n\n(J Clin Psychopharmacol 2015;35: 535–543)\n\nAttention-deficit/hyperactivity disorder (ADHD) is a prevalent and highly heritable neuropsychiatric disorder with childhood onset and at least partial persistence into adulthood in about 65% of cases.1 Attention-deficit/hyperactivity disorder in adults is associated with adverse outcomes in many domains including education, work, driving, self-esteem, social skills, obesity, substance misuse, and use of health care and social services.2 First, epidemiological studies indicate a substantially increased ADHD prevalence among prisoners (25%–45%) compared with the general adult population (2%–5%).3,4 Second, ADHD with or without concomitant conduct disorder, substance use, and antisocial personality disorder (ASPD) increases the risk of criminal offending.5–8 Third, criminal offenders with ADHD might be more difficult and expensive to manage and rehabilitate, likely related not only to earlier onset of criminality, higher recidivism rates, more frequent and severe intrainstitutional aggression, but also to psychiatric comorbidities including personality and substance use disorders (SUDs).9\n\nMethylphenidate is a first-line treatment for children and adults with moderate or severe ADHD.10 Most pharmacological trials were conducted with children, demonstrating short-term efficacy in improving ADHD core symptoms. However, more recent clinical trials have also confirmed short-term efficacy of stimulants and atomoxetine on ADHD in adults.11,12 Importantly, despite high rates of cooccurring psychiatric disorders in adults with ADHD, these trials regularly excluded individuals with coexistent disorders, substance misuse, and criminality.13,14 Longer-term effects and safety from medication treatment also remain uncertain. Hence, most importantly, clinical trials and naturalistic follow-up studies should address effectiveness and safety of ADHD medication also in these patients with multiple disadvantages.2,11,15 Methylphenidate pharmacotherapy for criminal offenders had, in fact, not been evaluated until recently.16 However, evaluating enduring effects of ADHD medication is challenging because of varying availability and continuity, including transition from child and adolescent psychiatry to adult services, as well as differences in the quality of services. Furthermore, the motivation of antisocial ADHD patients to participate in interventions may fluctuate substantially; discontinuation rates are high, resulting in variable treatment duration, and concomitant medication and coexistent psychopathology may confound or modify treatment responses.2,11,15,17,18 Finally, there are important ethical considerations related to withholding medication from control subjects in randomized controlled trials (RCTs) over prolonged periods.\n\nRecent studies suggest that current treatments reduce the negative impact of ADHD on adult global functioning including a substantial reduction of criminality.2,19 We evaluated long-term outcomes for adult male prisoners with ADHD and coexistent disorders in a prospective naturalistic follow-up performed 1 and 3 years after completion of a previously reported 52-week methylphenidate trial conducted in a Swedish high-security prison.16,20 We hypothesized that treatment gains observed during the initial 52-week trial would be maintained in subjects with continued ADHD medication at follow-up.\n\nMATERIALS AND METHODS\n\nSubjects\n\nAll 25 adult male prisoners that provided end point data in the 52-week trial were defined as completers and eligible to\n\nFrom the *Division of Psychiatry, Department of Clinical Neuroscience, and †Department of Medical Epidemiology and Biostatistics, Karolinska Institutet, Stockholm, Sweden.\nReceived February 3, 2015; accepted after revision July 7, 2015.\nReprints: Ylva Ginsberg, MD, PhD, Department of Medical Epidemiology and Biostatistics, Karolinska Institutet, Box 281, SE-171 77 Stockholm, Sweden (e-mail: ylva.ginsberg@ki.se).\nCopyright © 2015 Wolters Kluwer Health, Inc. The trial was conducted in 2007 to 2010 in a Swedish high-security prison for adult, male, longer-term inmates convicted almost exclusively for violent or drug-related offenses. The Ethical Board of Stockholm and the Swedish Medical Products Agency (MPA) approved the trial and the naturalistic posttrial follow-ups. The 52-week trial was independently monitored by the Karolinska Trial Alliance and inspected by the Swedish MPA to ensure adherence to Good Clinical Practice and the Declaration of Helsinki.\n\nAfter a comprehensive diagnostic evaluation (n = 34), 30 inmates diagnosed with ADHD received a thorough description of the study and provided informed consent for participation. Participating inmates were severely symptomatic and functionally impaired with high rates of coexistent psychiatric disorders (Table 1).\n\nThe 52-week trial comprised an initial 5-week randomized, double-blind, placebo-controlled, fixed-dose (72 mg daily) phase followed by a 47-week open-label extension during which all inmates received optimally titrated OROS-methylphenidate (maximum 1.3 mg/kg daily) alongside regular prison-based offender treatment programs within the prison setting.16,20 Medication was highly effective and safe overall in reducing ADHD symptoms, improving global and executive functioning, behavior control, and quality of life, both in the short term (5 weeks) compared with placebo (Cohen's d = 2.17; number needed to treat, 1.1), and over 52 weeks when delivered as part of multimodal treatment. No misuse of ADHD medication or side abuse of other drugs was detected by repeated, mandatory urine toxicology throughout the study period. We prospectively followed up completers of the 52-week trial, 1 and 3 years after trial to elucidate long-term outcome.\n\nDemographic Information\n\nA letter with information about the follow-up study (same procedure for the 1- and 3-year follow-up) was sent to the completers of the 52-week trial, together with the self-administered rating scales described later, a questionnaire addressing current medication, reasons for any treatment discontinuation, prison release, reoffending, employment status, and current support from society. In line with our hypothesis, the substantial improvements in ADHD symptoms, global symptom severity and functioning, and quality of life observed during the trial were maintained after up to 4 years with continued methylphenidate treatment. Continued medication at the 3-year follow-up versus not was associated with fewer ADHD symptoms, less functional impairment, lower alcohol and drug use, and increased quality of life.\n\nThe long-term effects of ADHD medication in adults with this disorder have been mostly unknown, because of short duration of clinical trials and few long-term observational studies. Nevertheless, our positive outcomes from a total of 4 years of methylphenidate treatment are in line with recent studies suggesting that adequate treatment may reduce the negative impact of ADHD on everyday life functioning in adults2 and that medication for more than 2 years was associated with more symptomatic and functional improvement than medication for shorter periods.31 Moreover, a recent Swedish register-based total population study indicated a decrease in substance abuse with ADHD medication; the longer duration of ADHD medication, the lower the rate of substance abuse.32\n\nCoexistent disorders, adverse effects, and insufficient follow-up routines have been associated with poor adherence and treatment discontinuation.31,33 In this study, lack of proper follow-up was reported as a reason for discontinuation, together with poor effectiveness, adverse effects, and individual strategies for coping with ADHD that made medication unnecessary. We also found that participants who improved more in self-reported ADHD symptoms and quality of life during the trial were more likely to remain on medication at the 3-year follow-up. This is consistent with a Swedish study of nonoffender patients by Bjerot et al33 reporting that a robust treatment response during the first year of ADHD medication predicted adherence to medication at 2-year follow-up. However, the authors also reported that unknown or unspecified reasons for stimulant treatment discontinuation were frequent. A retrospective, naturalistic Norwegian study addressed predictive factors for more than 3 years of stimulant treatment among adults with ADHD.14 Despite high levels of coexistent disorders, including substance misuse and antisocial behavior, a median of 33-month duration of stimulant treatment was reported, suggesting the feasibility of also treating patients with complex psychopathology. Furthermore, the use of methylphenidate with extended-release formulations (ER) correlated with longer treatment duration. This is in line with Ramos-Quiroga et al34 (2008) who suggested that the convenience of ER compared with immediate-release formulations contributed to increased compliance and less treatment discontinuation. Although the Norwegian study used much lower methylphenidate doses (mean, 58.9 mg daily) than ours, the positive long-term outcomes from OROS-methylphenidate treatment (an ER formulation) in criminal offenders with ADHD agree with their findings.\n\nWe also observed increased daily doses during follow-up, from a median of 108 mg after trial via 142 mg at the 1-year follow-up to 144 mg at 3 years. Notably, the doses reported by participants at follow-up assessments were higher than those usually recommended by guidelines.10 First, one possible explanation to the observed increased doses could be optimized dose titration as most participants were not fully remitted by the end of the trial, thus leaving room for further improvement that clearly tapered off between the 1- and 3-year follow-ups. This possibility is supported by a recent OROS-methylphenidate study with criminal offenders diagnosed with ADHD and concurrent amphetamine dependence.35 Participants were mainly treated in outpatient care after release from prison and usually required daily methylphenidate doses of 144 to 180 mg for improvement. This suggests that ADHD with concurrent SUD might require higher methylphenidate doses than recommended by the NICE guidelines,10 possibly related to down-regulated brain dopamine systems and increased tolerance to stimulants.36 As mentioned previously, all our participants had lifetime histories of SUD. A second possible reason for increased doses might be higher demands on the individual when leaving the highly structured prison environment. Life outside prison requires higher levels of executive and independent functioning, areas that usually are impaired in ADHD.\n\nThe proportion of released participants was 75% at the 3-year follow-up. Most respondents reported support from various authorities and caregivers; yet, 67% were in full- or part-time work at the 3-year follow-up. The high employment rate might be considered a favorable outcome, given our sample of highly symptomatic and functionally impaired severe criminal offenders,21 especially considering that ASPD is a negative predictor for long treatment duration in ADHD patients.14 During 3 years of follow-up, 20 of a total of 25 trial completers taking part in follow-up assessments were released from their index prison sentence.\n\nIn most countries, criminal reoffending is quite common after release from prison. During the study period, relapse rates reflected in new registered arrests or suspected offenses of any sort within 3 years were approximately 70% in Sweden for formerly imprisoned men with violent crime(s) as principal offense, and approximately 67% for those with a principal drug-related offense.37 In our sample, 7/14 subjects (50%) with a violent principal offense and 1/6 (17%) of those with a principal drug-related offense self-reported reoffending throughout the 3-year follow-up. The offense types included car driving without a license, illegal drug use, fraud, illegal threats, and assault. Albeit imprecise because of few observations, these comparisons indicate substantially lower relapse rates among our respondents compared with similar male clients released from prison during the same period. However, considering again the few participants in the present study, our results need to be interpreted with caution.\n\nNevertheless, the promising effect sizes and persistence over time for ADHD medication suggested from this first prison trial with long-term follow-up and the high prevalence of ADHD in criminal offenders indicate substantial potential to reduce the individual and societal burdens of criminality.3,21,38,39 However, this would require cooperation between psychiatric, addiction, correctional, and social services to provide linked services for offenders with ADHD, involving continued support and, preferably, multimodal interventions.\n\n© 2015 Wolters Kluwer Health, Inc. Hence, the results of this study should be generalized with caution to other offender populations. Some additional limitations also need consideration here. First, we were primarily interested in evaluating longer-term effects of participation in the initial 52-week methylphenidate trial. For this reason, we only included trial completers. It would also have been of interest to explore differences in long-term outcome between dropouts and completers of the trial. However, the limited sample size and resulting low statistical power did not allow such comparisons. Second, a few completers could not be traced or declined to participate in the 1- and 3-year follow-ups. However, participation rates of 96% and 80%, respectively, might still be regarded as high considering our complex study population of severe criminal offenders with ADHD and SUD. Third, although we relied primarily on observer-rated outcomes, self-report measures were also used. Yet, a previous study reported that adults with ADHD reliably rated current symptoms,40 despite a risk of underestimating ADHD-related impairments.41 Fourth, data on medication were self-reported by participants at the 1- and 3-year follow-ups, and no data were collected from the prescribing psychiatrists regarding medication dosages, tolerability and safety, frequency of visits, or if motivational support or nonpharmacological treatment was provided. Fifth and finally, results should be interpreted with caution because this was a naturalistic follow-up study without an untreated control group. However, our results should add both to the currently sparse literature on long-term effects of ADHD medication (4 years in total) and on potential long-term benefits from treatment of the underserved population of criminal offenders with ADHD and SUD.\n\nCONCLUSIONS\n\nWe conducted a long-term, follow-up study of ADHD medication in criminal adults with considerable comorbidity, patients otherwise regularly excluded from RCTs. Symptomatic and functional improvements gained during a 52-week OROS-methylphenidate trial in adult male, long-term prisoners with ADHD and substantial concurrent psychopathology including substance misuse seemed to persist up to 3 years after trial completion (exposed to 4 years of treatment in total). Importantly, most clients were also employed at the 3-year follow-up and had not relapsed into criminal offending or substance misuse. Together with observations of poorer outcomes for participants untreated at follow-ups, this indicates that improved routines and collaboration to ensure continued motivation, support, and treatment might reduce individual and societal injury from criminal (re)offending. Additional studies are warranted to validate these preliminary findings in new and larger samples.\n\nACKNOWLEDGMENT\n\nThe authors thank the participants and all collaborators from the Stockholm County Council and the Swedish Prison and Probation Services who made this work possible.\n\nAUTHOR DISCLOSURE INFORMATION\n\nThis was an investigator-initiated study funded by the Swedish Ministry of Health and Social Affairs and the Stockholm County Council, Sweden.
